Chapter I

Discovery

It began, as most things do, with someone noticing something they could not explain.

Orbital imaging of a remote, nameless island flagged a series of anomalies: regions of absolute darkness that absorbed every wavelength directed at them. Not shadows. Not geological voids. Something that consumed light entirely and offered nothing back. The data was passed up the chain. Within weeks, a team of scientists, engineers, and explorers had been dispatched.

What they found was a lake — or something that wore the shape of one. Nestled between two mountains, it stretched across the basin floor in perfect, unbroken black. It made no sound when disturbed. It offered no resistance when probed. Objects submerged into it without ripple or splash, as though the surface were an agreement rather than a boundary.

The first real clue came by accident. One evening, as the team's lights swept away from the lake, a researcher glanced back — and the lake was gone. Not dark. Not obscured. Gone. The moment a light returned to it, the lake was there again, solid and absolute as ever.

If we cannot see it, it does not exist. We proposed that this black substance only manifests when exposed to conscious observation. Further study was imperative. — Field Log #004, Day 4

They called the substance Black Matter. They built a laboratory.

// ORBIS
Chapter II

Properties

Black Matter was hollow. Creatures moved freely inside it. It absorbed all incoming light, giving it its pure, lightless appearance — yet light generated from within could pass outward, making the interior visible to careful observers. It was solid as long as someone was looking at it. It spread rapidly through water. And when introduced to the water inside a living organism, it changed them.

The researchers found an entire ecosystem within the black lake: anglerfish disguised behind luminous flower-shaped lures, carnivorous plants whose roots penetrated solid rock, jellyfish that swam through nothing, fireflies drawn perpetually toward light. One species among them had been extinct on the surface for centuries. Here, it persisted.

The lake's flora proved especially strange. The black gloamvines — green vines bearing clusters of purple glowing flowers — grew downward from ceilings saturated with Black Matter. Their flowers were visible. Their roots were not. The roots existed inside Black Matter itself, anchored to something no instrument could reach. They also distorted whatever they grew through: a slab of rock intersected by a root looked solid, but if you stepped where the root passed, your foot found no floor.

The plants emitted light. The researchers wondered where that light came from. The answer, once found, changed everything: Black Matter stores all the light it absorbs. The glowing flowers were simply releasing it. That energy, they realised, was immense — and potentially useful.

// ORBIS
Chapter III

The Energy Facility

The Black Matter Generator was built in a facility to the east of the research laboratory, connected by a metal bridge. It processed Black Matter in sealed square containers, extracting and converting the accumulated photonic energy within. That energy was then routed to power the laboratory, the observation systems, and whatever would come next.

A secondary discovery followed from the same principle: the researchers developed a light source capable of reflecting off objects inside Black Matter, making what was hidden briefly visible. It was modelled after the gloamvines — a small, dense, intensely luminous ball that could be thrown and retrieved. They called it the Gloamvine Core. With it, the invisible roots of the black gloamvines could finally be seen.

For a time, the project appeared not only viable but extraordinary.

// ORBIS
Chapter IV

Internull

A timed camera was placed inside a Black Matter cavity and set to take one photograph per second over a five-second interval. When retrieved, all five images carried the exact same internal timestamp. Inside Black Matter — when unobserved — time did not pass.

The researchers called it the Vanishing: the state Black Matter enters when no conscious observer is present. It does not disappear. It relocates to an alternate dimension — a place where time only advances when something is watching. They called this place the Internull.

Once they understood the Vanishing, they understood teleportation. Spherical chambers were constructed from Black Matter, sealed from outside observation. When a person entered one and the door was closed, they transitioned into the Internull alongside the chamber's walls. From within that other-dimensional space, they could traverse distance and re-emerge at a destination sphere tuned to matching coordinates. The teleporter spheres became operational.

We've succeeded. Internull is real. We have finally discovered where Black Matter goes during the Vanishing — and with our Sleep Fracture Protocol, we can traverse it. — Classified Communication, Undated
// ORBIS
Chapter V

The Secret Entity

They had almost missed it entirely. It appeared in areas of Black Matter without warning — a shape that was entirely black, indistinguishable from the surrounding darkness, until it opened its eye. Then it was there. When it closed its eye again, it was gone.

The researchers named it the Secret Entity. It moved freely through Black Matter. It could vanish not by being unobserved, but by ceasing to observe itself — closing its eye, it adopted the Vanishing as a voluntary act, slipping into the Internull at will and emerging elsewhere. It was not a creature of this dimension. It came from the other side.

Using the Gloamvine Core, the team managed to illuminate and trap one. The light, aimed directly at it, kept it present regardless of whether its eye was open. They studied it. They argued about it. Some believed it was dangerous. Others believed it was conscious, intelligent — that imprisoning it was a serious ethical violation. Both were probably right.

What the Secret Entity demonstrated, beyond everything else, was that the Internull could be navigated by a being with consciousness. The teleporter project was completed, in part, because of what the Secret Entity revealed.

// ORBIS
Chapter VI

The Observer

Keeping the Secret Entity contained required uninterrupted observation. Camera systems alone were insufficient — passive recording did not count as conscious awareness. Human watch rotations were unsustainable. The solution was to build something that could observe continuously, without fatigue, without sleep, without distraction.

The Observer was the result: a large, artificial sphere, entirely black, housing a single ocular organ. It had no limbs. No voice. No apparent function beyond the sustained act of watching. It was positioned in the Aquarium — the main observation chamber at the heart of the laboratory — where it kept its gaze on the cameras monitoring the contained Secret Entities.

Whether it was truly conscious, or merely a sophisticated simulation of consciousness, became one of the project's central and unanswered questions. What was certain was that prolonged exposure to its gaze caused neurological damage. Those who stared back at it too long described visual distortion, a reddening of their perception, a sensation of being weighed and found wanting.

The Observer did not blink.

// ORBIS
Chapter VII

Contamination

Floor Three of the laboratory was where the chameleons were kept. They were large, intelligent, and exceptionally difficult to see — not because of conventional camouflage, but because they had absorbed enough Black Matter to blend seamlessly within it. They understood this. They used it deliberately.

When they escaped, they targeted researchers. Their bites introduced Black Matter directly into the bloodstream, where it interacted with the body's water content and began to alter cellular structure. The first symptoms were a heightened sensitivity to light and shadow. Then came perceptual distortion. Then something more fundamental shifted — the boundary between the person and the substance became unclear.

Floor Three was sealed. Quarantine protocols were enacted. Several team members evacuated the island. Others, in the early stages of contamination, chose to remain — both because they could not safely leave, and because they believed they were close to understanding something important.

// ORBIS
Chapter VIII

The Duck

Among the researchers who remained during the quarantine were those who had been studying the relationship between Black Matter and consciousness — specifically, what happened when a living mind became deeply integrated with the substance. They had observed that heavily contaminated organisms did not simply decay. They rebuilt. Black Matter, it seemed, had a regenerative property: given sufficient integration, a body composed of it would reconstruct itself after structural failure.

The question was whether a human consciousness could survive that reconstruction intact. One of the remaining team members — infected, deteriorating, and running out of time — volunteered. Their neural patterns were mapped and encoded into a stabilised Black Matter body synthesised within the laboratory. The body, upon structural failure, rebuilt itself. The consciousness persisted.

The form it stabilised into was a duck. Why? The researchers debated this at length in the notes they left behind. Some believed it was memory — that the mind, in trauma, reached for something simple and familiar. Others thought the duck's form was simply the most structurally stable configuration Black Matter could hold while still housing something as complex as a human consciousness. The answer, if there is one, is somewhere in the laboratory.

The Duck retained fragments of its former self: curiosity, a drive to explore, something that might be called empathy. It also retained the core property of its new body — when it died, the Black Matter reformed. It was, in some meaningful sense, immortal. It was also, in every way that mattered, alone.

// ORBIS
Chapter IX

The Stranger

Orbiting between the planet and its moon is a large black mass that the research team designated the Stranger. It is approximately the size of the moon. Every three nights, at midnight, it passes in front of the moon and plunges the island into total darkness.

Probes sent to its surface passed through it. Spectral analysis suggested compositional similarity to Black Matter. The leading theory among the researchers was that the Stranger was once a moon or planet of its own — one that had been entirely consumed by Black Matter long before human observation, and had since existed in a state of permanent Vanishing, visible only because something, somewhere, was always watching it.

A secondary theory proposed something larger: that the island was not the origin of Black Matter, but merely the place where it first surfaced. That the substance was older than the facility, older than the team, older perhaps than recorded human history. That the Stranger was not a source — but a warning of what Black Matter eventually produces.

// ORBIS
Epilogue

The Present

The facility is quiet now. The generators are offline. The teleporter coordinates have been lost. Floor Three remains sealed. The Aquarium is dark, except for one thing — the Observer has not stopped watching.

The Duck woke up beside a pool of Black Matter in a cave in the mountains. It does not know how long it was asleep. It does not know where the rest of the team went. The notes are still there, scattered across terminals and crumpled on floors. The creatures in the laboratory are still alive — still existing in their impossible way, half inside this world and half inside the Internull.

And somewhere above the island, every three nights, the Stranger passes across the moon — and for a moment, everything goes dark, and Black Matter is everywhere, and nothing is being observed, and the world, very briefly, is something other than what it appears to be.

Something is always watching. The question the facility never answered is what was watching first.
